Section 01
Why Writing
Matters
Why exposition matters in olympiads
Core Principle
An olympiad solution is not a transcript of your thoughts. It is a finished mathematical argument whose job is to convince a grader.
  • Correct mathematics is the foundation.
  • Structure tells the grader where the real idea is.
  • Presentation affects how much of your real progress the grader can actually see.
  • Long solutions do not automatically earn more credit.
A Useful Mindset
Write for a reader who is intelligent, busy, and seeing your idea for the first time.
What a grader wants to know quickly
A grader should identify these almost immediately
  1. What is the final answer?
  2. What is the main idea?
  3. What is the hard step?
  4. Why does the solution finish after that step?
If these are hidden, the proof feels weaker than it really is
Even a correct solution can look incomplete if the key claim is buried inside a paragraph, if the equality case is never stated, or if the argument ends without explicitly returning to the problem statement.
What weak write-ups usually look like
  • They begin calculations before stating the goal.
  • They use notation before defining it.
  • They bury the key idea in the middle of a large paragraph.
  • They mix hard steps with routine steps so the reader cannot tell which is which.
  • They stop after proving something close to the problem, but not the exact target.
Important
A poor write-up often reveals not just poor style, but incomplete understanding of the proof itself.
Section 02
Universal Architecture
of a Strong Solution
Master solution template
Solution.
Answer: ...

Let ...

Claim: ...

Proof.
  Step 1: Key observation
  Step 2: Main argument
  Step 3: Deduction

Hence ...
Therefore ...

□
  • Answer: gives the destination immediately.
  • Let … introduces notation before it is used.
  • Claim: isolates the bridge from assumptions to conclusion.
  • Steps: force a logical order on the argument.
  • Hence / Therefore: explicitly close the proof.
The universal flow
  1. State the answer or exact target.
  2. Define the objects and notation.
  3. Tell the reader the main idea.
  4. Prove the crucial claim or lemma.
  5. Carry out the easier deductions cleanly.
  6. Return to the original problem statement and conclude.
Why this structure works
Most olympiad proofs consist of one decisive insight plus a number of routine verifications. Good writing separates those two layers.
Crossing the ocean
The Central Idea
A strong olympiad solution contains one step where the proof truly moves from "promising calculations" to "actual solution".
  • In geometry, this may be the similarity or cyclic quadrilateral.
  • In algebra, this may be the key identity or substitution.
  • In combinatorics, this may be the invariant or extremal choice.
  • In number theory, this may be the decisive modulus, valuation, or descent.
Writing Advice
When you cross the ocean, put that step on its own line. Name it. Display it. Prove it as a self-contained claim.
Useful sentence patterns
High-Value Phrases
  • "We claim that …"
  • "The key observation is …"
  • "It suffices to prove …"
  • "We first show attainability."
  • "It remains to eliminate all other cases."
  • "Now we prove this is a lower bound."
  • "Therefore the only possible value is …"
Why These Help
These phrases are not filler. They signal the logical role of each paragraph.
Section 03
Special Structures:
Find All & Extremal
How to write a "find all" solution
Recommended Order
  1. We claim the only solutions are …
  2. We verify these candidates satisfy the conditions.
  3. We prove there are no other solutions.
Very Common Mistake
Many students only derive candidates and stop. That is not the same as proving those candidates are the only possibilities.
Best Final Sentence
Therefore the complete set of solutions is {…}.
How to write a minimum/maximum solution
Recommended Order
  1. We claim the minimum/maximum is …
  2. We prove this value is attained.
  3. We prove this value is a lower/upper bound.
Why This Matters
A bound alone is not an extremum. An example alone is not an extremum. You need both parts.
Strong Ending
Hence the minimum is …, attained when ….
Attainment, equality cases, and verification
  • If the answer is a number, say when equality holds.
  • If the answer is a family of objects, verify that family explicitly.
  • If the verification is easy, still include it.
  • The problem asked for a complete solution, not only the hard half.
Example Style
We claim the minimum value is 16. This is attained at
x₁ = x₂ = x₃ = x₄ = 1.
We now show no smaller value is possible.
Section 04
Definitions, Notation,
and Visual Structure
Definitions are part of the proof
  • Definitions must be complete and precise.
  • If an object matters repeatedly, give it a name.
  • If the definition is complicated, include a quick example.
  • In geometry or combinatorics, the diagram and the definitions should match perfectly.
Danger
A typo in a definition can destroy the whole argument, because the reader has no reliable way to repair your intended meaning.
How to introduce notation properly
Weak Style
"Consider the bad set and the special vertex and the important line."
Strong Style
Let B be the set of bad indices, let v be a vertex of maximum degree, and let ℓ be the line through P parallel to BC.
  • Named objects reduce repetition.
  • Good notation forces you to think clearly about what is actually defined.
  • Reusing notation consistently makes long proofs much easier to read.
Leave space
  • Skip lines between substantial paragraphs.
  • Put claims and lemmas on their own lines.
  • Display important equations.
  • Split casework into separate blocks.
  • If a calculation is long, use aligned displays instead of prose.
Reason
Dense formatting makes even correct mathematics feel suspicious. Whitespace is not cosmetic; it reveals structure.
Diagrams are not optional
  • If your solution used a diagram, the reader should get one too.
  • This is not only for geometry.
  • In combinatorics and game problems, pictures can be decisive.
  • A missing diagram forces the grader to reconstruct your exact configuration.
Practical Rule
If the proof depends on where points, cells, edges, or regions are located, include the diagram.
Section 05
How Much Detail
to Include
How much detail is enough?
Reliable Criterion
Include enough detail that a strong student who did not solve the problem could not plausibly have written the same solution.
  • Routine algebra may be compressed.
  • The decisive step may not be compressed.
  • If a sentence contains the actual difficulty of the problem, it needs proof.
  • If you cite a theorem, explain how it applies here.
Too little detail vs. too much detail
Too Little
  • "Clearly"
  • "By symmetry"
  • "Worst case"
  • "Easy to see"
  • "The result follows"
These are dangerous if the omitted step is not truly routine.
Too Much
  • Repeating the same definition many times
  • Expanding trivial computations line by line
  • Writing every thought in discovery order
  • Explaining standard algebra that is not the heart of the proof
The real problem is often disorganization, not excess detail.
Write forwards, not backwards
Preferred Logical Flow
X ⟹ Y ⟹ Z
Avoid This Shape
"We want Z. If Y were true then Z would follow. But X is equivalent to Y, and X is given, so Z."
  • Forward writing is easier to read.
  • Forward writing makes hidden assumptions easier to detect.
  • Forward writing usually shortens the final solution.
Citing known results
  • It is usually fine to cite a named theorem that does not trivialize the problem.
  • If the quoted result is not completely standard, outline why it applies.
  • Do not choose between "prove everything from scratch" and "say nothing".
Good Style — Cauchy–Schwarz Example
By Cauchy–Schwarz,
(a₁² + ⋯ + aₙ²)(1 + ⋯ + 1) ≥ (a₁ + ⋯ + aₙ)².
Applying this to the sequence …, we obtain …
Section 06
Writing
Algebra Solutions
What algebra solutions need most
  • A clear target expression or target family of solutions
  • A named substitution or normalization
  • A highlighted key identity
  • A clean separation between the non-routine insight and the routine manipulations
  • Explicit equality cases or verification
Normalize
→
Structural identity
→
Apply inequality / factorization
→
Analyze equality
Algebra opening template
A Good Start
We claim the minimum value is M, attained at …

Let
x = a/b,  y = b/c,  z = c/a.
The key observation is the identity
F(a,b,c) = G(x,y,z).
Once this is proved, the inequality follows from AM-GM.
  • The answer is stated.
  • The normalization is introduced.
  • The heart of the proof is telegraphed.
Inequality solutions in algebra
  1. State the claimed bound and equality case.
  2. Decide whether the problem wants homogenization, substitution, smoothing, or direct inequality tools.
  3. Extract the one formula that makes the rest work.
  4. Only then do the routine estimates.
  5. End by checking equality carefully.
Common Mistake
Students often write three pages of manipulation and never indicate which transformation was the intended turning point.
Equations and functional equations
  • In equations, isolate the factorization or symmetry that kills cases.
  • In functional equations, list the strategic substitutions intentionally.
  • If a value such as f(0) or f(1) is central, name it early.
  • Number especially important displayed equations if you will reuse them.
Functional-Equation Style
Set x = 0 to obtain (1).
Set y = 0 to obtain (2).
Subtracting (1) and (2), we deduce …
Algebra checklist
Did you state the answer early?
Did you identify the main substitution or identity?
Did you avoid expanding blindly when a factored form is stronger?
Did you verify the equality case or all candidate functions?
Does the solution explain why the chosen method was natural?
Section 07
Writing
Combinatorics Solutions
What combinatorics write-ups need most
  • Precise definitions of the configuration
  • Named sets, graphs, color classes, or moves
  • A visible invariant, extremal object, or counted quantity
  • Separated casework
  • A clean count or contradiction at the end
Core Idea
In combinatorics, definitions are part of the proof. If the model is unclear, the proof is unclear.
Combinatorics opening template
A Strong Start
Let X be the number of ordered pairs (A, B) such that …
We count X in two ways.

Alternatively:
Choose a configuration maximizing the number of red edges. We claim this configuration has no isolated red vertex.
  • The parameter is named.
  • The proof method is visible immediately.
  • The reader can already anticipate the shape of the argument.
Double counting, invariants, and extremal arguments
Double Counting
State: what set is counted; how the first count works; how the second count works; what identity or inequality results.
Invariants
State the quantity and then prove it stays fixed or changes monotonically.
Extremal Arguments
Name the extremal object explicitly: a largest clique, a smallest counterexample, a maximum matching, a minimum bad set.
Casework in combinatorics
Weak Format
One paragraph containing all possibilities in discovery order.
Strong Format
  1. Case 1: The chosen vertex lies in the set S.
  2. Case 2: The chosen vertex lies outside S.
Conclude with one sentence explaining why these cases are exhaustive.
Key Rule
At every moment the reader must know what is fixed, what varies, and what is being counted.
Combinatorics checklist
Are all sets, vertices, edges, colors, moves, and bad objects defined?
Is the invariant or extremal choice stated explicitly?
Is the double count presented in two distinct pieces?
Is every case visibly separated?
Is the final contradiction or final count written explicitly?
Section 08
Writing
Geometry Solutions
What geometry solutions need most
  • A usable diagram
  • Exact definitions of points, lines, circles, and intersections
  • A clearly announced target relation
  • A highlighted key lemma: usually similarity, cyclicity, homothety, or concurrency
  • A finish that returns exactly to the asked statement
Geometry Principle
In geometry, the reader should never wonder where a point lies or what a symbol refers to.
Geometry opening template
A Good Start
Let M be the midpoint of BC, let ω be the circumcircle of ABC, and let X = AM ∩ ω with X ≠ A.

We claim that B, C, M, X are harmonic, and hence it suffices to prove
∠BXM = ∠MCA.
  • The construction is precise.
  • The target is visible.
  • The reader knows which step is going to matter.
Avoiding the angle-chase blob
Weak Style
"Thus ∠A = ∠B = ∠C = ∠D, so everything follows."
Strong Style
∠ABX = ∠ACX
because A, B, C, X are concyclic.

Also,
∠ACX = ∠ADX
because C, D, A, X are concyclic.

Hence
∠ABX = ∠ADX,
so A, B, D, X are concyclic.
Geometry checklist
Is there a usable diagram?
Are all constructed points defined exactly once?
Did you state what you are trying to prove before chasing angles?
Is the decisive similarity or cyclicity claim isolated?
Does the final sentence match the problem statement exactly?
Section 09
Writing
Number Theory Solutions
What number theory solutions need most
  • The right prime divisor, modulus, or extremal choice
  • Explicit notation for valuations, orders, gcds, and divisibility
  • A visible obstruction: parity, congruence, descent, or valuation
  • Clean separation of the key contradiction from routine arithmetic
  • Explicit verification in "find all" problems
Choose modulus / prime
→
Force structural rigidity
→
Contradiction or classification
Number theory opening template
A Strong Start
Assume for contradiction that a solution exists, and choose one with minimal n.

Let p be the smallest prime divisor of n. We claim that
p = 3.
Once this is shown, the remaining argument is routine.
  • The contradiction setup is explicit.
  • The extremal choice is justified.
  • The key claim is visible before the arithmetic begins.
Congruences, valuations, and descent
Congruences
Say why a modulus is natural. Choose mod 4, 8, 9, p, p² or n for a structural reason, not by random trial.
Valuations
Introduce vp(n) early and explain what exact valuation you are trying to prove.
Descent
"Choose the smallest counterexample" is not decoration; it is the engine of the contradiction.
How to write contradiction cleanly
Standard Contradiction Template
Assume for contradiction that there exists an integer n with property P.

Choose such an n with minimal value.

We show how to construct a smaller integer m < n with the same property.

This contradicts the minimality of n.
Why This Is Good Writing
The extremal choice explains why the next move matters. Without it, the proof can feel like random algebra.
Number theory checklist
Did you explain why that modulus or prime was chosen?
Did you define vp, gcd, or ordp before using them?
Did you isolate the key contradiction?
Did you verify all claimed solutions in a classification problem?
Did you end with the exact final answer?
Section 10
Examples of
Strong Phrasing
Useful sentence stems — Algebra & Number Theory
Algebra
  • "We homogenize the inequality by setting …"
  • "The key identity is …"
  • "Equality holds exactly when …"
  • "It remains to verify that the candidate family indeed works."
Number Theory
  • "Let p be the smallest prime divisor of n."
  • "Reducing modulo 8 gives …"
  • "Applying LTE yields …"
  • "This contradicts the minimality of n."
Useful sentence stems — Geometry & Combinatorics
Geometry
  • "It suffices to prove …"
  • "We claim that these four points are concyclic."
  • "Now triangles PQR and XYZ are similar because …"
  • "Hence the required collinearity follows."
Combinatorics
  • "Let X denote the number of …"
  • "We count X in two ways."
  • "Choose a configuration maximizing …"
  • "This quantity is invariant after each move."
Section 11
Common
Failure Modes
Mistakes that cost points
  1. Writing false claims and planning to repair them later
  2. Skipping the main difficulty with words like "clearly"
  3. Missing one direction in a "find all" or extremal problem
  4. Using undefined notation
  5. Citing a theorem without saying how it applies
  6. Doing casework without showing the cases are exhaustive
  7. Ending with a lemma rather than the original target
Blunt Truth
The majority of unexpectedly low-scoring solutions are genuinely incomplete or incorrect, not merely badly styled.
Suspicious phrases
  • "By symmetry, assume …"
  • "In the worst case …"
  • "It is obvious that …"
  • "One can easily show …"
  • "The rest is routine."
When Are These Allowed?
Only when the omitted step is truly routine and not where the problem lives.
Safer Alternative
Replace vague compression with one crisp sentence that explains the actual reason.
Fake progress
  • proving only special cases
  • assuming what must be shown
  • asserting the key lemma without proof
  • deriving a candidate answer without checking it
  • proving a bound without showing attainability
  • giving a construction without proving optimality
A Good Self-Check
Ask: have I solved the exact problem, or only something nearby?
Section 12
Revision
and Checking
How to revise a draft
  1. Rewrite the solution neatly.
  2. Look for the main claim and move it into a visible place.
  3. Merge repetitive calculations.
  4. Split giant paragraphs into logical units.
  5. Check every definition and every equality case.
  6. Make the ending explicitly answer the question.
Revision Principle
Many "too long" solutions do not need deletion; they need reorganization.
A practical contest-time checklist
Did I state the answer clearly?
Did I define every symbol?
Is the hardest step visible?
Are all cases covered?
For "find all" or extremal problems, did I do both directions?
In geometry or configuration problems, is the diagram usable?
Does the final sentence say exactly what the problem asked?
Final Contest Habit
Write clearly and carefully enough that you can catch your own mistakes while writing.

Do not merely compute residues; explain what obstruction those residues are meant to create.
Section 14
Closing
Summary
Final philosophy
A Strong Olympiad Solution Is Not Just Correct
It is arranged so that the grader sees, in order:
  1. the answer
  2. the main idea
  3. the key claim
  4. the clean deduction to the final result
One-Line Takeaway
State the destination early, highlight the bridge that gets there, cross it cleanly, and stop exactly at the required conclusion.
